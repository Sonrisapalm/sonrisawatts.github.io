Emotion Encoded Research Initiative: AI in the Classroom

Parents, Teachers, and Artificial Intelligence in the Classroom — Trust… but with Conditions

As part of my research initiative Emotion Encoded, I asked participants a simple but important question:

“Would you be comfortable if your child’s teacher used AI to help with grading or lesson planning?”

The responses depicted something powerful:

  • 53.7% said “yes,” but only under conditions. Parents were open to AI as long as it performs its task and the teacher reviews every assignment and decision. This suggests parents prefer AI as an assistant rather than a replacement—highlighting the value placed on human reasoning, rationale, and judgment.
  • 26% said “no.” This group expressed resistance, showing a lack of trust in AI for something as rigorous and formative as their child’s education. One possible reason: a child’s education requires critical thinking and emotional intelligence—capacities many believe AI has not mastered.
  • 11% said “yes” without conditions. For this group, AI was viewed as another helpful tool in the classroom.

Conditional trust for AI in the classroom signals a bigger message: for effective AI integration into educational systems, psychology matters.

How Cognitive Biases Shape Parent Attitudes

Status Quo Bias

Definition: A preference for current systems over change, even when change may be beneficial.

Classroom example: “We’ve always graded manually.” Parents may feel similarly about traditional school tools and processes.

Automation Bias

Definition: Over-trusting AI outputs while discounting human judgment.

Risk to avoid: Teachers blindly accepting AI-generated grades without scrutiny.

Algorithm Aversion

Definition: Rejecting AI advice even when it is statistically superior.

Possible link to the data: The 26% who reject AI entirely may be driven by distrust of algorithmic decision-making in sensitive contexts like education.

Framing Effect

Definition: Acceptance changes depending on how an option is presented.

Insight from this study: Parents tolerate “AI-assisted” grading but are more likely to reject “AI-automated” grading.

This echoes a larger theme in my research initiative: AI is not simply about performance; it’s about how people emotionally negotiate trust with these systems.
