Fluency Bias & The Law: Insights from Attorney Jonel Powell

For the latest expert series at Emotion Encoded, I spoke with Attorney Jonel Powell to get his perspective on what happens when clinical instinct meets a computer program. We explored the reality of legal liability and why "Explainable AI" is actually just a new way for experts to get tripped up in a small country.

The Trap of "Convincing" Logic

When we talked about the danger of "convincing" AI, Jonel was clear that the perceived intelligence of the machine can be its most dangerous trait.

"As convincing as an explanation may seem, one should always verify the veracity of the explanation from a trusted authority particularly if you do not understand it."

This is exactly what I call "fluency bias." AI is great at sounding smart and logical, which can trick even a pro into turning off their own brain. In a small place like St. Kitts, if an AI says something "convincing" but you can't back it up with a local authority, you're walking into a trap.

Efficiency as Bait

Legal research in the Caribbean is notoriously time-consuming. This makes the "shortcut" of AI an incredibly tempting proposition for busy firms.

"AI provides a fast and sometimes 'shortcut' approach in the legal field, which can entice lawyers to rely on the perception of its efficiency, sometimes to their detriment and even that of their client."

If a lawyer starts choosing the fast way over the deep dive, the client is the one who ends up paying for that "efficient" mistake.

The Need for "Island Intelligence"

Perhaps the most critical point Jonel made was about the lack of local context. AI models are trained on global data, often ignoring the nuances of Caribbean legal systems.

"It would be foolhardy to solely rely on AI in determining a legal dispute as it lacks the human elements of discretion and sensitivity. The underavailability of our local legal resources can also lead to incomparable substitutions by the AI resulting in flawed reasoning and outcomes."

Because Caribbean law isn't all over the internet like US or UK law, the AI just starts guessing. It uses "incomparable substitutions"—foreign logic to solve a local problem.

As we bring these tools into the region, the goal shouldn't be to replace a lawyer's gut feeling, but to build systems that actually respect it. Without a human judge to add in actual sensitivity and local context, we risk trading justice for a digital summary that is totally flawed for our reality.
