How AI Can Support Teaching Without Replacing the Teacher

Dr Sarah Owen on the benefits, risks, and practical advice for using AI in Caribbean classrooms

Dr Sarah Owen reflects on how AI can change teaching and learning when used thoughtfully. Her perspective emphasizes guidance, cultural fit, and preserving the human contribution to education.

AI as a starting point, not a replacement

Dr Owen explains that AI can provide an efficient starting point for lesson planning and personalized learning. She warns that AI draws on existing online content and may fail to capture regional or cultural nuances relevant to Caribbean classrooms. Her core recommendation is to use AI outputs as foundations that educators adapt and enrich with their professional expertise.

“

AI can provide a useful starting point but experience and expertise in curriculum development should ensure plans match student needs and cultural context.

— Dr Sarah Owen

Benefits and challenges

Top benefits

  • Alleviates writer's block and jumpstarts lesson planning.
  • Saves time on preliminary research and resource discovery.
  • Supports personalized learning pathways when guided by teachers.

Key challenges

  • Risk of promoting a lazy approach when students submit AI-generated work unvetted.
  • Potential spread of misinformation when outputs are not carefully checked.
  • Homogenization of thought and cultural mismatch if tools are not localized.

Advice for teachers

Dr Owen encourages teachers to embrace AI while maintaining professional oversight. Her practical tips stress customization, verification, and turning AI into a scaffolding tool that enhances critical thinking rather than replacing it.

  • Start small and experiment with specific tasks such as drafting lesson outlines.
  • Always customize and verify AI content for accuracy and cultural relevance.
  • Teach students to use AI as a prompt and to build on it with original thinking.

"Use AI to set the foundation but build on it with your own learning, critical thinking, and unique perspective."

— Dr Sarah Owen

Conclusion

Dr Owen's guidance frames AI as a powerful assistant that must be curated by teachers who understand their students. For Caribbean education systems, localization and teacher-led adaptation are essential to prevent cultural erosion and to ensure meaningful learning outcomes.

Conclusion

Dr. Owen emphasizes balance: embrace AI for efficiency but safeguard pedagogy, culture, and student agency. For Caribbean education systems, that balance requires intentional adaptation, teacher training, and policy guidance that preserves local knowledge while responsibly integrating new tools.
